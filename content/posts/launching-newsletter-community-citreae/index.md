Little over 6 years ago I started my first serious side project: [juice news](https://juicenews.com). Knowingly, I made the mistake of saying that that project would be "free, forever". And while it yielded revenue indirectly, there were numerous moments and indicators that a project alike could and should have been monetized.

But I am a man of my word. And while curating juice news, along the way I saw new opportunities arise. One of them for an even more niche market than the juice & beverage industry... namely the **citrus** industry.

## Launching [citreae.com](https://citreae.com)

On August 10th of 2020 I launched my 2nd free newsletter. This one with different focus and with a different road map. When the first edition was sent out, I had the 2nd and 3rd phases beyond the newsletter lined up and prepared. Because of that, I could quickly shift gears and come to a situation 2 months later where I had the first (pre)paying customers.

### Pre-launching the citreae platform

With the cross pollination from juice news came credit. Something I hoped for, but something that needed validation.

When I reached 200 subscribers, I decided to validate the idea for the *platform* by pre-launching it:

- I shared my plans for what the platform would look like
- I stated the added value and sett if off against the competition
- I gave the time line for the different phases (pre-launch, soft-launch, launch)
- I immediately gave the option to buy a Lifetime Membership at a hefty discount (€39 instead of €199)
- I made it clear that this was a once in a lifetime opportunity that would not return
- I reiterated that the deadline for the discount is set in stone

I sent out the announcement and went out for run. When I came back 45 minutes later... I was amazed to see that the first paying customer was there. I sold a lifetime membership 23 minutes after announcing it. *Boom!*

> Ok, this is the *maker* speaking. I mean, yes, I do have a business as a broker where paying customers are a thing and have been. But when you have created something new, something different and out of you comfort zone (not so much the audience, but more the product for which clients do pay), then the sale has a total different value. The number is not what it is about... it is what it stands for. The commitment (even up front as in this case) is an enormous validation and compliment. And for me it is the proof that the potential within this market, industry is there.  
> And honestly, that gives a jolt of joy. No matter how small it may seem.

In the hours and days after, more sales came in and further strengthened that I was on the right way. Besides the number of sales it was also the quality of the paying members. I know my industry pretty well, know the players that make a difference. And when you see those names, those organizations show support... that means a lot.

### Building the platform

With the newsletter I had a couple of things already in place: Although I probably switch at later stage to another ESP, for now my beloved Mailchimp is doing its job providing the audience with daily news and separate updates on what's going on behind the scenes.  

For the backend content management and preparation, there is mainly airtable and zapier, in combination with some tools tailored over the years to minimize my labor... call it semi-automation.

The other outer facing services (memberships, community, directory/resources) that were needed to be added required some research. The communities of Makerpad, Indie Hackers and Product Hunt were the immediate go-to for this... But man... too many options. Research, reading comments, testing different separate options... getting fed up and looking for all inclusive solutions (also there no perfect solution... Man, there are still gaps in the market people!!)

Eventually got time breathing in my neck and decided to settle for Outseta, Circle and Webflow. It needed some creativity and concessions, but I think that I am happy with the outcome. Well. for now.

There still is room for optimization, but for me the most important was that I needed stuff up and running in order to receive new clients.

### Ambitions vs Execution vs Time

Due to relations I had built up over time and some *street cred* in the juice industry, I did some promotional exchanges with other publishers in the same arena. One of the planned cross promotions was timed perfectly, but I made an ambitious guess about when I would reach the deadlines I had set for myself. *Oh boy. Do **not** do that.*

Thing is, that when setting deadlines for yourself AND you make them public, there is not only pressure, but you are setting a tone and an expectation with others... based on nothing.
